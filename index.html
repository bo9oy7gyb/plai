Proposal to acquire Plai.

Asset Purchase structure. Eight sections covering the deal and the integration plan.

This is Infillion's proposal for the acquisition of Plai Technology Inc. It contains the Letter of Intent, the deal terms map, the integration plan, the operating plan that Logan's sign-on is scored against, and the supporting analysis behind each. The deal is structured as an Asset Purchase under DGCL §271 with all 19 outstanding Plai SAFEs assumed by Infillion at full Purchase Amount and a voluntary Equity Conversion Right granted to each SAFE holder at Infillion's Series D close. Plai operates post-close as the SMB BU under the Plai brand. Logan joins Infillion as GM of the SMB BU and a member of the Infillion Management Team.

The deal in one paragraph

Infillion acquires substantially all of Plai's assets and business in an Asset Purchase. Plai's common stockholders (Welbaum ~95% / Gohara ~5%) receive $100.00 in cash collectively as the residual distribution following the SAFE waterfall. Plai's 19 outstanding SAFEs (aggregate Purchase Amount $3,123,808) are assumed by Infillion — Infillion steps into Plai's role as issuer, with the SAFEs continuing per their original terms with limited modifications: triggers reference Infillion events, §1(d) priority is adjusted to fit Infillion's capitalization, and auto-conversion at the next Infillion Equity Financing produces the §1(b) Cash-Out Amount. Each SAFE holder is additionally granted a non-transferable Equity Conversion Right triggered by the closing of Infillion's Series D Financing, allowing voluntary surrender of the Assumed SAFE for Series D-2 Preferred Stock at a Conversion Price fixed in the Definitive Agreement. Plai's approximately $1.1M in balance-sheet cash funds Logan's sign-on bonus, wind-down costs of the residual Plai entity, and any additional pre-close obligations identified during diligence; the Acquire.com transaction fee is paid by Infillion directly. Logan joins as GM of the SMB BU and a member of the Infillion Management Team — receiving strategic direction from the Executive Chair (Rob Emrich), operational direction from the COO (Brian), and financial accountability to the CFO, with a Functional Manager assignment confirmed before offer-letter finalization. His employment package — base salary continuity, an annual performance bonus (structure to be mutually determined during diligence), and an NQSO equity grant — is detailed in the Management Incentive Plan (Section V) and is the retention mechanism, separate from the de minimis deal consideration to common stockholders.

IISection II — How Infillion and Plai fit together

How Infillion and Plai fit together.

What Plai brings to Infillion, what Infillion brings to Plai, and why the combination works.

This section explains why the deal makes structural sense. It pulls from Plai's existing business, Infillion's existing platform, and the Integration Plan in Section VI to set up the context the rest of the proposal sits inside. There is no rhetorical case being made; the question is whether the technical fit, the customer-base fit, and the team fit are real enough to justify the structure the LOI proposes.

What Plai is today

Plai is a $1.8M ARR SaaS business with roughly 400 active accounts serving approximately 10,000 customers. The product is an autonomous AI ad creation and optimization platform: a customer enters a URL, and Plai generates ad creative, copy, audience targeting, channel mix, and budget pacing across 11 walled-garden channels (Meta, TikTok, LinkedIn, Google, Spotify, YouTube, Snap, Pinterest, X, Reddit, Bing). The autonomous optimizer runs hour-by-hour, shifting budget between channels based on performance. The team is small — Logan, the COO, and a tight engineering and CS group with prior experience at Google Ads and Meta Ads.

What Infillion is today

Infillion is a profitable advertising platform expected to do over $300M globally in 2026, across over 1,000 customers and seven consecutive years of profitability. The company is organized into three Groups — a Go-to-Market Group (the revenue engine), a Platform Group (shared infrastructure that compounds across all BUs), and a Corporate Group (the functional service units that enable the whole system: Finance, Office of the Executive Chair, Corporate Operations, and Legal). The GTM Group contains the commercial Sub-Groups: Media BU (brands, agencies, local media customers, and agency-infrastructure customers, on a fee model), RMN BU (retail media networks, on a margin model), and the Data BU formed through the Foursquare acquisition currently closing in parallel (GM pending). The Platform Group contains the Pillar Sub-Groups: Data, Supply, Demand, Creative, and the In-Store Operations Pillar (joined post-Catalina). Infillion has not raised equity since 2018 and has built its current position primarily through M&A — 11 acquisitions over the last 10 years that contributed components now composing into the platform.

Infillion today, with the Foursquare acquisition closed. The full interactive placemat — including the four moves that integrate Plai — lives in Section VI.

The structural principle that connects the Groups is composability. Platform Group components — built once, surfaced via APIs and shared services — are composed by each BU's Product & Tech team into finished products for that BU's customer type. The same Platform components compose into many BU products. The 80/20 read: roughly 80% of any customer's needs are served by the same underlying Platform components; the BU's P&T team produces the remaining 20% as the finishing layer. This is the architectural reason Infillion can run M&A as a primary growth strategy without rebuilding the playbook for each acquisition — every acquired person is absorbed by being assigned a Team Lead, a Functional Manager, and a Charter, and every acquired component lands in a Pillar where every BU draws on it.

What Infillion does not currently have is an SMB-segment Sub-Group inside the GTM Group. The Media BU and RMN BU are structurally built around customer relationships with brand managers, agency traders, and retail-media principals at $100K+ annual spend. SMBs spending $10,000/month or less are a customer set those BUs are not built to serve. The Plai customer base — roughly 400 active accounts reaching approximately 10,000 customers — sits in exactly that segment, and Plai will keep its own brand.

How Plai fits inside Infillion

The Infillion playbook for M&A is that the GTM part of an acquired company becomes a Business Unit — or is added to an existing BU — and the product and tech are moved into the Platform Group under the appropriate Pillars. Product & Tech teams also stay in the BU so it can move quickly — the same engineers who built the technology continue to ship against the BU's roadmap, while the components themselves are surfaced as Platform capabilities that every BU draws on. In the case of Plai, we may keep the whole product and tech team within the SMB BU.

Plai becomes Infillion's SMB BU — a new Sub-Group within the GTM Group, operating under the Plai brand. The brand stays — existing Plai customers wake up on the same login, the same workspace, the same pricing, the same product. The Infillion association is announced in customer communications but the brand reading remains Plai.

What Plai adds to the platform

Plai's technical components distribute across the Platform Group Pillars. The text-to-ad generator and AI creative tools join the Creative Pillar alongside TrueX, IDVx, and the Ad Studio. The 11 walled-garden channel connectors (Meta, TikTok, Google, LinkedIn, Spotify, YouTube, Snap, Pinterest, X, Reddit, Bing) join the Supply Pillar alongside MediaMath supply and our direct publisher integrations. The unified allocation engine, cross-channel orchestration logic, and decisioning AI join the Demand Pillar, where they can be used by any BU's P&T team. The cross-channel performance data the agent generates feeds the Data Pillar and is added to the combined data pool the Data BU monetizes externally.

What Plai takes from the platform

The reverse flow is equally material. Plai gains access to the programmatic channels available through MediaMath that are not currently buyable through Plai — web display, connected TV, mobile in-app, and DOOH. Plai also gains access to the audience, identity, and measurement capabilities in the Data Pillar — our XGraph, Foursquare place visits, Catalina purchase data. These are capabilities Plai's 400 active accounts cannot get standalone.

The structural thesis

The net result is that Infillion is using audience, identity, and measurement to establish a control point with the end customer that makes them think about walled-garden supply as just another supply source. Inventory they can correlate to a unified ID (through XGraph), target across the open web (CTV, DOOH, web display, mobile in-app) as well as the walled gardens, and ultimately measure which inventory source or channel performs best. The walled-garden supply that Plai brings becomes inventory inside a larger composable picture, not a siloed product. The same allocator decisions get made across a much larger menu of supply, against a unified measurement plane. The Integration Plan in Section VI walks through each move on the placemat.

The proposal that follows takes that strategic fit and works it through the deal mechanics. Section III sets out the proposed Letter of Intent. Section IV walks through the structural items behind it, item by item, and closes by addressing why those terms work for each party.

Four blocks. The first — Asset Purchase + SAFE Assumption — is the structural framework that defines the transaction. The remaining three — Wind-Down Reserve, Material Contract Assignability, and Employment Agreements — are the items most likely to surface friction during diligence or definitive drafting.

Structural framework vs. parameters. The Asset Purchase structure with SAFE Assumption and voluntary Equity Conversion Right is the operative deal framework. A. Asset Purchase + SAFE Assumption

The structural framework that defines this transaction. The reasoning is decomposed below as A.1, A.2, and A.3.

A.1, Asset Purchase Structure

Position: Asset Purchase under DGCL §271, with Plai retaining certain liabilities and continuing as a shell corporation through orderly wind-down. Infillion acquires substantially all of Plai's assets and business at closing. Common stockholders receive $100.00 collectively as the residual distribution.

Why: The $100.00 to common is mechanical: in a structure where SAFE holders receive their full Purchase Amount via the Assumed SAFEs (Section A.2), there is by definition no residual for common stockholders to participate in beyond the de minimis closing distribution. Logan's economic alignment lives in the employment package (Section V — Management Incentive Plan), structured to scale with the SMB BU's performance.

Flexibility: Limited on the Asset Purchase choice itself; substantial on the Material Contract Assignability fallback. If material contracts cannot be assigned during diligence, the parties restructure as an equity acquisition on economically equivalent terms. The fallback is structural insurance, not a hard preference, and either path is closeable. See C, Material Contract Assignability below.

A.2, SAFE Assumption

Position: All 19 outstanding Plai SAFEs ($3,123,808 aggregate Purchase Amount) are assumed by Infillion at closing. Infillion steps into Plai's role as issuer. The Assumed SAFEs continue per their original terms with three limited modifications: triggers reference Infillion events; §1(d) liquidation priority is adjusted to fit Infillion's capitalization; auto-conversion at the next Infillion Equity Financing produces the §1(b) Cash-Out Amount rather than the §1(a) Conversion Amount.

Why (for SAFE holders): As a standalone entity, Plai may never reach a valuation that makes the SAFE holders whole. With $1.1M in balance-sheet cash against $3,123,808 in SAFE Purchase Amount, an immediate shutdown and liquidation today returns roughly 33¢ on the dollar to SAFE holders, before wind-down costs. The Plai path to delivering the full Purchase Amount requires growing the business to a valuation event large enough to clear the SAFE waterfall — which is possible but uncertain. Under the proposed structure, the §1(b) Cash-Out Amount equals the original Purchase Amount in full, and every SAFE holder's economic floor is preserved by default. At Infillion's current valuation, the Conversion Amount under each SAFE's §1(a) mechanics is below the Cash-Out Amount across all tranches — so all 19 holders are in the §1(b) regime regardless. Holders can either take the Cash-Out at the next Infillion Equity Financing or elect into the Equity Conversion Right (§A.3); the choice is theirs and the terms are visible before the decision.

Why (for Infillion): The SAFE Assumption is a real obligation. Infillion assumes $3.12M in SAFE Purchase Amount; if the business fails, Infillion is still on the hook for the Cash-Out Amount when the next Equity Financing closes. The structure is not a cost-free way to acquire Plai's business — it is a deliberate transfer of the SAFE-holder risk from a $1.1M-cash startup to Infillion's balance sheet, on the bet that the combined platform produces the operating result that makes the assumed liability look small.

Flexibility: Limited on the assumption framework itself; flexible on the specific drafting of the §5(d) and §5(a) consent solicitations, on the timing of the consent process within the diligence window, and on how key SAFE holder relationships (TenOneTen, Hawke Ventures, Crush Next, Elbaz Trust, Y Combinator) are individually approached and managed.

A.3, Equity Conversion Right

Position: Each SAFE holder is granted a non-transferable Equity Conversion Right at closing, exercisable upon the closing of Infillion's Series D Financing. 30-day election window. Holder surrenders the Assumed SAFE in exchange for Infillion Series D-2 Preferred Stock equal to Purchase Amount divided by the Conversion Price. Conversion Price is fixed in the Definitive Agreement and is independent of the Series D-1 per-share price.

Why: The Equity Conversion Right is the upside layer on top of the preserved §1(b) floor. SAFE holders who want to participate in Infillion's next equity round can convert; those who prefer cash can keep their Assumed SAFE. The Conversion Price independence from the Series D-1 per-share price is the structural device that delivers meaningful conversion upside — the negotiated reference price will be set materially below the Series D-1 PPS, so holders who exercise the Conversion Right receive more equity per dollar of Purchase Amount than the Series D-1 investor receives at the headline round price. That spread is what makes the conversion attractive and what aligns the SAFE holder base with Infillion's medium-term equity story.

Flexibility: Substantial on the Conversion Price negotiation, on the election window mechanics, and on the specific drafting of the grant document. The framework — non-transferable contractual option, 30-day window at Series D close, surrender-and-receive mechanic, Conversion Price independent of D-1 PPS — is the structural commitment.

B. Wind-Down Reserve & Plai Cash

Plai carries approximately $1.1M in balance-sheet cash at signing. That cash supports the operational closing of the deal: Logan's sign-on bonus, the wind-down costs of the residual Plai entity, and the broader transactional expenses.

B.1, Cash Allocation

Position: Plai's approximately $1.1M cash is allocated as follows: more than half funds Logan's sign-on bonus (the cash component of his employment package — see Section V, the Management Incentive Plan); 10–20% funds the wind-down costs of the residual Plai entity (counsel fees, final payroll for non-transferring contractors, software cancellations, Delaware annual report and dissolution filings, final tax preparation, customary cushion); and the remainder, if any, supports any additional pre-close obligations identified during diligence. The Acquire.com transaction fee is paid by Infillion directly, not from Plai's cash.

Why: Plai's cash is deployed where it produces the most useful outcomes for the deal. Funding Logan's sign-on bonus from acquired Plai cash rather than from Infillion treasury keeps the operator alignment economically clean: Logan's retention is paid from the value of the business he's joining Infillion to run. Wind-down costs cover the orderly dissolution of the residual Plai entity. The Acquire.com fee is a transactional expense Infillion takes on directly — keeping the Plai cash dedicated to operational and people-related outcomes.

Flexibility: Substantial on the specific dollar amounts. Working assumption is ~$600K to Logan's sign-on, ~$110K–$220K to wind-down (sized in good faith based on Plai's actual wind-down obligations), with the remainder available if needed for items surfaced during diligence (for example, contractor-classification exposure under California AB 5, longer-tailed SaaS subscriptions, customer disputes that need to be cleared pre-close). The framework — Plai cash funds sign-on and wind-down; Infillion pays the Acquire.com fee — is the structural commitment.

C. Material Contract Assignability

The gating workstream of the diligence plan. Logan's go-forward employment package: base, bonus, and equity. The structure that gets him paid for the operating outcome of the SMB BU.

Layer 1: The House Account model

The House Account is the largest single revenue lever in the Year-1 plan and the work that takes the longest to build. The mechanic is straightforward: Plai today sends customer ad spend directly to the walled-garden platforms on agency-tier or reseller terms. Under Infillion's broader media footprint, Infillion has the volume to negotiate House Account arrangements with each channel partner — directly with the platform, on terms that capture a margin on the channel spend that flows through Plai's connectors. The margin is what funds Layer 1.

The commercial work is channel-by-channel and sequenced over the first three quarters post-close:

  • TikTok — first 4 months post-close. TikTok's reseller and managed-service programs have the most flexibility for new partners. Logan's existing TikTok relationships and Infillion's broader spend volume should produce a House Account on reasonable terms within the first fiscal quarter.
  • Meta — first 6 months post-close. Meta's Marketing Partner Program tiers require a longer qualification process and more spend documentation. Combined Plai + Infillion volume meets the threshold; the work is procedural rather than commercial.
  • Google — first 9 months post-close. Google's Premier Partner status and managed-account arrangements take the longest to qualify but produce the strongest economics once in place. The work runs in parallel with TikTok and Meta, but the contractual close is the slowest.
  • Other channels (LinkedIn, Spotify, YouTube, Snap, Pinterest, X, Reddit, Bing). Sequenced after the top three based on volume. Several of these channels' partner programs are lighter-touch than the big three; the work is operational, not strategic.

Once a House Account is in place on a given channel, every dollar of customer spend that flows through Plai's connector on that channel produces a margin contribution to the SMB BU P&L. The margin scales with channel volume, which scales with the existing Plai customer base plus the new customers Plai acquires plus — eventually — the Media BU and RMN BU customers whose spend can be routed through these same connectors (per the integration architecture described in Section VI).

Layer 2: Existing customer repricing

The largest, fastest, lowest-risk lever in the plan is Layer 2 — and it doesn't require any new customers. Plai's ~400 existing accounts currently pay a SaaS subscription against the workspace. Inside Infillion, the same customers gain access to the unified allocator (Demand Pillar), MediaMath's open-web inventory paths (Supply Pillar), Data Pillar audiences and identity, and the cross-channel measurement plane.

The repricing motion is straightforward: at customer renewal, existing accounts transition from a workspace-subscription model to an orchestration-fee model that captures a platform fee on the managed spend routed through the allocator. The customer experience is unchanged — same login, same workspace, same product — but the revenue model captures more of the value the platform delivers. The $3M+ Year-1 target assumes a partial adoption rate (not every account renews to the new model in Year 1; some stay on the legacy subscription); the longer-term opportunity is materially larger.

Layers 3, 4, 5: Targeting, Measurement, Creative attach

Layers 3, 4, and 5 are attach motions against the repriced Layer-2 customer base. They are the Platform Group's existing capabilities surfaced through the Plai workspace and priced as add-ons.

Layer 3 — Targeting attach. Data Pillar audiences and identity signals (XGraph identity resolution, Foursquare place visits, Catalina purchase audiences, MediaMath performance data) attach to a Plai customer's campaigns. The customer adds an audience layer to the workspace and the allocator routes spend against it. The pricing is a per-thousand-impression data fee on top of the orchestration fee.

Layer 4 — Measurement attach. Attribution, brand lift, and foot-traffic measurement attach to the customer's reporting. The Plai workspace already shows campaign performance metrics; Layer 4 expands those metrics to include cost-per-store-visit (via Foursquare/Catalina), cost-per-purchase (via Catalina), and cross-channel brand lift studies (via the measurement infrastructure inherited from Catalina and Foursquare). Pricing is a per-study or per-month measurement fee.

Layer 5 — Creative attach. Plai's text-to-ad generator already produces creative assets. Inside Infillion's Creative Pillar, the same generator (now exposed to every BU) produces creative assets that get cross-sold to Media BU customers as a service Plai's tech enables. Year-1 target captures both the SMB BU's direct creative attach and the Media BU cross-sell.

Layer 6: SaaS subscription growth

Layer 6 is net-new logo growth on the existing SaaS subscription model. Plai's customer acquisition runs on two motions:

  • Dogfood inbound (existing). Plai's content marketing and dogfood approach (running Plai's own ad campaigns on Plai's stack to acquire Plai customers) is the existing customer-acquisition engine. Continues unchanged inside Infillion.
  • Phase-2 franchise outbound (new). Multi-location franchise customers — the segment Plai already serves well — get an outbound motion built around the SMB BU's existing anchor customer base. The pitch combines Plai's core product with Infillion's full-stack capabilities (CTV, foot-traffic measurement, retail-media) for franchise customers whose value proposition is "drive store visits at scale."

Year-1 target: $2.5M+ of net-new subscription revenue on top of the $1.8M baseline.

The break-even mandate

Logan inherits a business running ~$30K/month burn against ~$100K/month operating expenses on $150K/month MRR (≈$1.8M ARR). The mandate at close: run the SMB BU at break-even or better by the end of fiscal Q2 post-close.

Break-even is measured on a fully-loaded BU P&L basis — SMB BU revenue covers SMB BU operating expenses (direct costs, team comp, infrastructure, the BU's share of allocated Pillar and shared-service costs per Infillion's standard methodology). Pre-close opex of ~$100K/month is the starting line; Logan himself identified the path to ~$50K/month sustainable opex. That is the working target.

The break-even discipline is the structural commitment to operating the SMB BU as a real Business Unit rather than as a perpetually-subsidized growth project. It does not mean stopping investment in growth — the Year-1 plan calls for substantial revenue scale-up across the six layers above — but it does mean that the BU's revenue has to cover its costs within the first two quarters of operating inside Infillion. The deal thesis depends on that discipline.

What Logan must reduce

The path Logan owns: identify which roles, vendors, tools, marketing spend, and contractor relationships in Plai's pre-close $100K/month run-rate are duplicated by Infillion's existing capabilities, redundant given the strategic refocus, or not worth carrying at break-even discipline. The integration plan supports that path through several mechanisms:

Shared services come from Infillion at no marginal BU charge for Year 1. Finance, HR/People, Legal, IT, security, and corporate communications are absorbed by Infillion's existing infrastructure for the first twelve months — these costs come off the SMB BU P&L. Year 2 onward they re-allocate per Infillion's standard BU charge-back methodology.

Engineering composability dividend. Plai's CTV / programmatic / retail-media / geofence / DOOH integrations are no longer build-it-yourself line items; they come from existing Infillion components (TrueX, MediaMath, Catalina, Gimbal, Foursquare, InStadium). Engineering headcount Logan was about to hire to build these doesn't get hired.

Channel rebates fund Layer 1. The House Account model generates margin on existing customer media spend that flows into the SMB BU P&L — partially funding the BU through customer media volume rather than subscription revenue alone.

The Q1 tripwire

If the SMB BU is not on a clear break-even trajectory by end of Q1 post-close (90 days), the Executive Chair and CFO trigger a joint review with Logan to either accelerate cost reduction, slow specific investments, or — if the gap is structural — escalate to a recut of the Year-1 plan. Opex discipline is its own bar; the Q1 review happens against that bar.

This is not a tripwire in the sense of "you miss it, the sign-on claws back." The clawback in Section V §A is mechanical: voluntary resignation without Good Reason or termination for Cause within 24 months. The break-even review is operational — a forcing function for the joint Logan/CFO/Exec Chair review that produces a recut plan, not a termination event.

A working framework, not a contract

The operating plan above is the deal team's current working assumption. The specific channel mix, the order in which the layers are activated, the investment cadence, and the exact structure of the GTM motion will evolve as the SMB BU operates and learns what the market wants. The underlying claim — that the SMB BU generates ~$15M+ of net-new Year-1 revenue across the six-layer stack on top of Plai's existing $1.8M ARR — does not change.

Treat specifics as working hypotheses; treat the structural shape as the commitment.

How the plan ties to compensation

The MIP in Section V is engineered against this operating plan. The sign-on bonus in §A addresses the 24-month integration window. The NQSO grant in §D captures Logan's medium-term equity stake in Infillion's combined platform outcome, with Year-4 outcomes scaling against the multi-year revenue trajectory described above. The annual performance bonus in §C is part of the package; its specific structure — what it is tied to, how it is measured, how it pays out — is open and to be mutually determined during due diligence, calibrated against the operating outcomes the SMB BU is being asked to deliver against.

VIII.2, Logan's Role and Reporting Structure

Logan joins as GM of the SMB BU and a member of the Infillion Management Team. This subsection details what those two roles mean inside Infillion's operating system — the matrix structure that applies to every employee, the GM-specific three-way directional structure, IMT membership, and the Functional Manager assignment.

The matrix structure (company-wide)

Every person at Infillion has two primary relationships, not one. A Team Lead directs daily work, owns the Charter and business objectives, and sets the what. A Functional Manager owns the person as talent — career development, hiring, performance reviews, retention authority, termination authority — and sets the who. These are two distinct kinds of authority, not "two bosses": Team Leads own work; Functional Managers own people. The distinction is critical because it means neither role has full unilateral power, and it means an employee can move between Teams (under different Team Leads, working on different Charters) while their career trajectory stays with one Functional Manager who has continuity on them as talent.

The structure is a deliberate departure from a traditional single-boss org chart. It exists because Infillion is genuinely organized around composability: Platform Group components are surfaced as APIs and shared services that compose into many BU products, and the matrix structure mirrors the same logic on the people side — talent is reusable across Teams the way components are reusable across BUs. The cost is more complexity in any single person's reporting picture; the benefit is that people can be redeployed across Teams without losing their career arc, and Teams can be reshaped without losing the people on them.

The GM role and the three-way directional structure

At Infillion, "GM" is a specific role with specific authority across the system. A GM owns a BU P&L. A GM acts as Super Team Lead for the BU — each Sub-Group within the BU has its own Team Lead, but those Team Leads operate under the GM's direction. Direct deference is given to the GM throughout the system on customer-facing decisions. The GM is the closest person at the company to the customer and bears accountability for the BU's performance.

GMs are also a special case within the company-wide matrix. In addition to the Team Lead / Functional Manager relationships every employee has, GMs receive directional input from three places:

  • Strategic direction from the Executive Chair (Rob Emrich) — SMB BU positioning, the composability roadmap with the Pillars, SMB-relevant M&A and partnerships, the multi-year platform thesis the BU operates inside
  • Operational direction from the COO (Brian) — day-to-day operating cadence, integration milestones, cross-BU dependencies, shared-services consumption framework
  • Financial accountability to the CFO — the consolidated company P&L, budget vs. actuals discipline, forecast reliability, and the capital allocation framework that determines what the SMB BU can spend on engineering, sales, and marketing

This is not three reporting lines but three distinct kinds of guidance that come from three senior leaders, each covering their domain. GMs are senior commercial leaders, and the three-way directional structure exists because strategy, operations, and finance each require senior coordination across the company. It also gives Logan three places to escalate cleanly when something needs to move fast, rather than funneling everything through a single reporting line.

Logan, as GM of the SMB BU, is Team Lead for the SMB BU's leadership team — directing daily work, owning the SMB BU Charter, the P&L, the Strategy, the Budget, the Product Roadmap, the KPIs, and the Opportunity Roster (the required artifacts for any BU-level Sub-Group). The GM role gives him "Super Team Lead" authority across the SMB BU's Sub-Group: any Team Lead within the SMB BU operates under his direction. The SMB BU's standing BU Product & Tech team — the "finishing line" that composes Platform Group components into the SMB BU's products — reports to Logan as Team Lead.

Functional Manager assignment

Separate from the three-way directional structure, Logan has a Functional Manager — the person who owns him as talent, with authority over his career development, performance reviews, retention, and termination. The Functional Manager relationship is not "another boss"; it is the talent-side counterpart to the Team Lead role, providing career continuity, performance feedback, and retention support distinct from the day-to-day work direction the three-way structure provides.

The specific Functional Manager assignment will be confirmed before offer-letter finalization. The candidate set is shaped by two considerations: the Functional Manager should be a senior leader with continuity on Logan's career arc across the multi-year horizon, and the assignment should be distinct from the three-way directional structure (so the talent-side relationship has independent standing). The Comp Committee approves the assignment alongside the rest of the employment package.

IMT membership

The Infillion Management Team is the company-wide executive leadership body. It meets weekly on a 60-minute cadence. Members include the CEO, the GMs of each BU, the Pillar Leads, and the senior Corporate functional heads (CFO, COO, General Counsel, Head of People, CRO, CTO, CPO). The IMT sets strategy, reviews performance across BUs and Pillars, allocates resources, and makes cross-BU decisions. Logan participates in those conversations as a peer GM, contributing the SMB BU's perspective on cross-Group questions and receiving the company-wide context that informs SMB BU decisions.
